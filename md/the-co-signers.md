# The Co-Signers

**When you co-sign someone's debt, their default becomes your default.**

---

## The co-sign

In lending, a co-signer is the person who guarantees someone else's debt. They do not take out the loan. They do not spend the money. They sign the paper — and in doing so, they make someone else's failure their own. If the borrower defaults, the co-signer pays. If the borrower's credit collapses, the co-signer's credit collapses with it. The co-signer's exposure is the borrower's exposure, assumed voluntarily, often for reasons that seemed reasonable at the time, and discovered to be catastrophic only when the bill arrives.

The defining feature of a co-sign is that it cannot be undone by wishing it away. You cannot un-sign. You cannot quietly remove your name from the document. The only way to protect yourself, once the ink is dry, is to ensure the borrower never defaults — which means protecting the borrower becomes indistinguishable from protecting yourself. The worse the borrower's position, the more desperate the co-signer's defense of it. This is not loyalty. It is arithmetic.

Parts I and II of this series documented a closing in progress — debts being settled in policy, in sanctions relief, in American blood — and the underwriting apparatus that made those debts enforceable. This piece examines the co-signers: the political party that guaranteed the borrower's obligations, and whose members are now discovering what every co-signer eventually discovers — that the borrower they guaranteed has no way to make good on the debt. He reached the top of the structure, but there is nothing at the top. No equity. No collateral. No exit. Just the obligation itself, compounding — and every co-signer who followed him up added weight to a position that was already unsupportable.

The behavior documented in this piece — the suppression of files that Congress voted to release, the silence on intelligence-sharing that killed American troops, the active protection of an arrangement that serves foreign creditors at the public's expense — is not explained by ideology, loyalty, or political calculation alone. It is explained by the same structural logic that governs every other relationship documented in this series: the parties protecting the arrangement are the parties with something to lose if it unravels.

This piece documents what they signed, when they signed it, and what they now stand to lose.

## I. The vote and the reversal

On November 18, 2025, the United States House of Representatives [passed the Epstein Files Transparency Act](https://www.npr.org/2025/11/18/nx-s1-5611438/epstein-files-bill-house-vote) by a vote of 427 to 1. The bill was the most publicly demanded piece of legislation of the session. It had bipartisan sponsorship, bipartisan support, and a near-unanimous mandate. Its lead sponsor, Rep. Thomas Massie, had fought the White House, the attorney general, the FBI director, the speaker, and the vice president to bring it to the floor.

The single no vote was a procedural protest. Every member of both parties who voted — every one — voted yes.

This is worth pausing on. In a Congress that cannot agree on a continuing resolution, on judicial nominations, on the naming of post offices — 427 members looked at this bill and concluded the same thing: the public wants these files, and the cost of being seen to oppose that is higher than any other consideration. The vote was not bipartisan in the way that highway bills are bipartisan, where the math is about allocations and districts. It was bipartisan in the way that only happens when every member in the chamber has determined that the political risk of voting no is existential.

They had all done the math. And they had all done it wrong. The vote was easy because they assumed the files would name the other side's people — or, at worst, figures distant enough from the caucus to be expendable. Transparency was free when you believed the cost would be borne by someone else. What they had not calculated was the possibility that the files would name their own.

### What the files contained

The first release, in December 2025, was [heavily redacted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epstein_files). Over 500 pages were blacked out. Sixteen files disappeared from the public database within 24 hours. Faulty redactions allowed the public to recover some withheld content. By January 2026, 3 million pages had been released, and the DOJ claimed compliance.

But the names had begun to surface. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick [was documented visiting Epstein's private island](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/a-list-of-powerful-men-named-in-the-epstein-files-from-elon-musk-to-former-prince-andrew) with his family in 2012 — contradicting his public claim that he had cut ties decades earlier. Lutnick had previously [called Epstein "gross" and "the greatest blackmailer ever"](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/1/31/bill-gates-elon-musk-howard-lutnick-face-new-scrutiny-over-epstein-ties) while insisting the relationship was long over. The emails showed otherwise. [At least half a dozen current Trump administration officials](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/justice-department/least-half-dozen-top-trump-administration-officials-appear-jeffrey-eps-rcna258749) appeared in the files. The president's own name appeared in contexts that his Justice Department would subsequently move to suppress.

The files also named people whose proximity to power is financial rather than electoral. [Epstein's daily schedules](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/bill-gates-elon-musk-epstein-files-what-documents-show/) showed planned island visits with Elon Musk — including a 2012 email in which Musk asked which night would be "the wildest party" — and scheduled meetings with Peter Thiel and Steve Bannon. Musk [posted on X](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/a-list-of-powerful-men-named-in-the-epstein-files-from-elon-musk-to-former-prince-andrew) that he had "REFUSED" to visit the island, a claim the [detailed email correspondence contradicted](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/03/politics/epstein-files-musk-lutnick-branson-emails). What makes Musk's position relevant to this series is not the island emails alone. It is the pattern that followed: the man whose name appeared in the files subsequently embedded himself inside the federal government through DOGE, spent hundreds of millions supporting the political infrastructure that protects the arrangement, and — in a brief public feud with the president — [posted on X](https://www.newsweek.com/epstein-files-update-musk-bannon-thiel-house-oversight-10791107): "Time to drop the really big bomb: @realDonaldTrump is in the Epstein files. That is the real reason they have not been made public." The feud resolved. The files remained suppressed. The reader may draw their own conclusions about what that sequence represents.

The exposure is not limited to one side. The files named figures across the political spectrum — from administration officials to Democratic donors, from tech billionaires to foreign dignitaries. A Slovak national security adviser [resigned within 24 hours](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/a-list-of-powerful-men-named-in-the-epstein-files-from-elon-musk-to-former-prince-andrew) of appearing in the files. The bipartisan scope of the names is the structural foundation for both this piece and Part IV — because the exposure does not discriminate, and neither does the incentive to suppress.

And then, in February 2026, NPR [revealed](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/24/nx-s1-5723968/epstein-files-trump-accusation-maxwell) that the DOJ had systematically withheld files mentioning the president — including over 50 pages of FBI interviews with a woman who accused Trump of sexual abuse when she was a minor. The accuser told the FBI that around 1983, when she was approximately 13 years old, Epstein introduced her to Trump, who she alleged sexually assaulted her. The FBI [interviewed this woman four times](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/05/nx-s1-5737562/justice-department-missing-epstein-files-trump). Only the first interview — the one that did not mention Trump — was included in the public release. The DOJ blamed a "coding error." After NPR's reporting, [16 additional pages were published](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/05/nx-s1-5737562/justice-department-missing-epstein-files-trump), including three interview summaries in which the accuser described the alleged assault in graphic detail. [DOJ officials who spoke with the woman found her credible](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/doj-releases-epstein-files-containing-sexual-assault-allegations-against-trump); the Miami Herald's Julie K. Brown reported they would not have interviewed her four times if they had not.

Thirty-seven pages remain missing. What those pages are expected to contain is specific: [handwritten notes from the interviews](https://www.thedailybeast.com/doj-sitting-on-documents-involving-13-year-old-trump-accuser/), a law enforcement report, license records, and — critically — [internal communications that would memorialize how the situation with the accuser was resolved](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/doj-releases-epstein-files-containing-sexual-assault-allegations-against-trump). That last category is the one that matters most for this series. The notes from the interviews are summaries of what she said. The internal communications are the record of what the government *did about it* — who was told, what was decided, and why the matter was closed without action. If those 37 pages document a decision to suppress a credible accusation of sexual assault by a minor against a man who would later become president, they are not missing pages. They are the most dangerous pages in the federal records system.

[Both the Republican and Democratic chairs of the House Oversight Committee](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/25/nx-s1-5726780/democrats-doj-epstein-files-trump-missing-pages) opened investigations. [CNN's independent review](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/24/us/epstein-files-trump-accuser-missing-files-invs) found that over a quarter of all FBI witness interview records catalogued in the Maxwell evidence logs do not appear in the public database. The missing pages are not an anomaly. They are part of a pattern — and the pattern tracks with the names.

### What didn't happen

Here is where the behavior diverges — and where the co-sign becomes visible.

The party that controls the DOJ is the president's party. The party that controls every committee with oversight authority is the president's party. The attorney general who [privately informed the president](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epstein_Files_Transparency_Act) that his name appeared in the files before the public saw them was appointed by the president and confirmed by his party's Senate majority. The institutional machinery that would investigate the suppression is held entirely by the people with the most to lose from what an investigation would find.

And that machinery did not move.

The caucus that voted for these files did not treat their suppression as a five-alarm institutional emergency. It did not convene emergency hearings. It did not issue subpoenas for the missing 37 pages. It did not demand the attorney general testify under oath about why the files were "incorrectly coded."

What the majority did instead was something every co-signer recognizes: it absorbed the cost. It let the story cycle. It allowed the framing to shift from "the DOJ suppressed evidence" to "a bureaucratic error is being corrected." It watched the 37 pages stay missing and said nothing that would force them into the light. No emergency hearings. No subpoenas for the missing records. No demand that the attorney general testify under oath. The committees that exist to provide oversight provided none.

Rep. Thomas Massie, who led the bill and named the officials who fought it — the president, the attorney general, the FBI director, the speaker, the vice president — deserves scrutiny, not canonization. Massie is a libertarian-wing member from a safe Kentucky district who has built his political identity on being the principled contrarian. He votes against his own leadership routinely. His district rewards the fight itself — which means the fight costs him nothing. He is not proof that defiance is possible within the party. He is proof that defiance is possible only when your seat does not depend on the party's infrastructure. He can afford to push because his voters want the push.

And push he has. After the suppression was revealed, Massie did not go quiet. He [requested a Special Master](https://khanna.house.gov/media/press-releases/reps-khanna-and-massie-call-appointment-special-master-compel-department) to compel the DOJ to comply with the law. He [reviewed the unredacted files](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/09/politics/unredacted-epstein-files-doj-congress) at the DOJ and told reporters he found "the names of at least six men that have been redacted that are likely incriminated by their inclusion." He [posted on X](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epstein_Files_Transparency_Act): "It was not a hoax, I cannot be bullied, I am not done, and this is why those in power are doing everything in their power to defeat me." He [demanded on the House floor](https://www.rollingstone.com/politics/politics-news/epstein-files-thomas-massie-calls-out-doj-1235521879/) that the DOJ explain why there have been no "charges, arrests or investigations" in the United States while foreign governments are arresting people named in the same files. When the president ordered the release of UFO files, Massie [called it](https://www.thedailybeast.com/gop-rebel-accuses-trump-of-ultimate-weapon-of-mass-distraction/) "the ultimate weapon of mass distraction" and added: "But the Epstein files aren't going away… even for aliens."

This is what the absence of a co-sign looks like. One member, from one safe seat, doing what every committee chair with subpoena power could be doing and is not. The contrast between Massie's behavior and the silence from the committee chairs who hold the actual levers of oversight is the co-sign made visible. He can name names on the House floor. They can issue subpoenas. He is doing his part. They are not doing theirs.

The 427–1 vote is the baseline. Everything that happened after it is the delta. And the delta is the co-sign becoming visible.

## II. The signatures — a ratchet with no release

The co-sign did not begin with the Epstein files. It began years earlier, and it accumulated the way all co-signs do — one signature at a time, each one reasonable in isolation, each one making the next one harder to refuse, and all of them together creating an obligation that cannot be unwound without destroying the co-signer in the process.

A co-sign is a ratchet. It turns in one direction. Every vote to protect, every confirmation to install, every silence maintained when speech was required — each one tightened the mechanism. And ratchets do not release.

### The precedent

The ratchet did not begin with this president. It did not begin in any single year. But there is a moment that established the specific mechanism this piece documents — the proof of concept that demonstrated what the party could do, what it would cost, and what the base would tolerate.

In March 2016 — months before any party had nominated a presidential candidate — the Senate majority leader [announced](https://www.npr.org/2016/03/16/470664561/mcconnell-blocking-supreme-court-nomination-about-a-principle-not-a-person) that the Senate would not hold hearings, meetings, or a vote on the sitting president's nominee to the Supreme Court. The seat had been vacant for weeks. The nominee was the chief judge of the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals — a centrist jurist who had previously been confirmed by the Senate with bipartisan support. The constitutional obligation was unambiguous. The majority refused to act.

The [eleven members of the Senate Judiciary Committee signed a letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merrick_Garland_Supreme_Court_nomination) pledging to withhold consent on any nominee. No senator in the majority broke ranks. The nominee waited 293 days — shattering the [century-old record of 125 days](https://www.npr.org/2016/09/06/492857860/173-days-and-counting-gop-unlikely-to-end-blockade-on-garland-nomination-soon) — and was never granted a hearing, a meeting, or a vote. The majority leader later [told a crowd in Kentucky](https://www.newsweek.com/mcconnell-says-biggest-decision-leader-was-blocking-merrick-garland-1711872): "One of my proudest moments was when I looked Barack Obama in the eye and I said, 'Mr. President, you will not fill the Supreme Court vacancy.'" He subsequently called it ["the most consequential thing I've ever done."](https://www.axios.com/2019/01/22/mcconnell-blocking-garland-most-consequential-thing-ive-ever-done)

This is not the first signature. There were signatures before it — the normalization of shutdowns as leverage, the weaponization of procedural tools, decades of escalation that made this moment possible. But this is the signature that proved the model. A constitutional process could be simply *refused* — not on legal grounds, not on procedural grounds, but as an exercise of raw majority power — and nothing would happen. No member would lose their seat. No convention would revolt. The base would reward it.

The lesson was structural: the machinery of government is a tool, and the cost of using it this way is zero, as long as no one breaks. That lesson is the foundation of everything that followed. Every subsequent signature was made possible because this one held.

### The second signature: impeachment

In February 2020, every senator in the president's party except one [voted to acquit](https://www.npr.org/2020/02/05/801695409/watch-live-trump-on-brink-of-acquittal-by-u-s-senate) in the first impeachment trial. The charge was abuse of power — leveraging military aid to Ukraine to pressure a foreign government into investigating a political rival. The evidence was extensive. The vote was party-line.

The one senator who broke ranks was [booed at his own state convention](https://amp.cnn.com/cnn/2021/05/01/politics/mitt-romney-utah-gop-trump/index.html). Delegates introduced censure resolutions. [County parties voted to formally censure him](https://www.abc4.com/news/politics/utah-republicans-vote-to-censure-senator-mitt-romney/). The state party narrowly voted the censure down — [711 to 798](https://www.axios.com/2021/05/02/mitt-romney-censure-vote-fails-utah-gop-vote) — but the message was delivered. He was called a traitor by the president on social media. The message to every other member of the caucus was unambiguous: this is what breaking ranks costs. Not discipline from above — rejection from below. The base itself becomes the enforcement mechanism.

This signature was more expensive than the first, but the ratchet made it affordable. The party had already demonstrated that refusing a constitutional obligation carried no cost. Acquitting on party lines — in the face of extensive evidence — was only one step further. Most of the co-signers probably believed they were making a pragmatic calculation. They were. They were also signing the paper.

### The third signature: January 6th and the second impeachment

On January 13, 2021, ten House members from the president's party voted to impeach for incitement of insurrection. In the Senate, seven members of the same party voted to convict.

The aftermath was systematic. Of the ten House members who voted to impeach: [four lost their primaries](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/liz-cheney-house-republicans-who-voted-to-impeach-trump-fates-primaries-2022/) to challengers endorsed by the president. Four more retired rather than face primaries they could not win. [Only two survived](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2024-election/two-republicans-voted-impeach-trump-jan-6-return-house-rcna179904) — both from districts with nonpartisan primary systems that diluted the party's enforcement mechanism. One of those two [announced his retirement](https://www.axios.com/2025/12/17/dan-newhouse-retire-trump-impeachment-jan-6) in December 2025. Eight out of ten, destroyed or driven out. The third-ranking member of the House caucus went from leadership to losing her primary by 37 points in less than two years.

This is the ratchet made visible. The party did not merely disagree with the members who broke ranks. It destroyed them — systematically, publicly, and as a warning. The mechanism was not subtle. It was not supposed to be. The cost of defection was made visible so that every future calculation would include it.

### The fourth signature: the nomination

In 2024, the party nominated a man with [34 felony convictions](https://www.npr.org/2024/05/30/g-s1-1848/trump-hush-money-trial-34-counts) for the presidency. Every delegate who voted for the nomination, every official who endorsed, every member who campaigned — each one co-signed the conviction. Not in the sense that they approved of it. In the sense that they attached their political futures to a person whose freedom depends on holding power.

This is the signature that cannot be explained as pragmatism. A party can block a Supreme Court confirmation and call it strategy. It can acquit a president and call it institutional judgment. It can reject an impeachment and call it due process. But nominating a convicted felon for the highest office in the country is a bet — a bet that the convictions will never matter, that the power will hold, that the exposure will remain contained. It is a co-sign on the entire debt, not a single installment. And if the borrower defaults, every person who cast that vote owns a share of what follows.

The absence of dissent is itself the evidence. By 2024, the ratchet had turned so many times that the cost of objecting to the nomination of a convicted felon was higher than the cost of endorsing him. No floor fight materialized. No delegate challenge gained traction. The convention was a coronation — and every delegate who participated signed the paper.

### The fifth signature: the confirmations

This is where the co-sign penetrates the machinery of government — and where the experience of anyone who has worked inside that machinery becomes essential to understanding how the arrangement is enforced.

The Senate confirms presidential appointees. Every cabinet secretary, every undersecretary, every deputy, every assistant secretary, every inspector general — each confirmation is a vote to place a specific person inside a specific part of the federal apparatus. And each confirmed appointee becomes what the federal workforce calls a "political" — a political hire who sets direction for the career staff beneath them.

The significance of this for the arrangement described in this series cannot be overstated. The DOJ's suppression of the Epstein files was not executed by the attorney general personally. It was executed by the system she directs — by the career staff who process, categorize, redact, and release documents according to the direction set by the politicals above them. The "coding error" that caused 50 pages of FBI interviews to be withheld did not require a conspiracy. It required a directive — or even an understood priority — flowing from the political layer into the operational layer. In government, the political gives direction. The system follows. That is not corruption. It is how the structure is designed to work. The corruption is in what the direction is designed to protect.

Every senator who voted to confirm the attorney general co-signed whatever the attorney general does with that authority. Every senator who voted to confirm the FBI director co-signed the bureau's handling of the files. Every senator who voted to confirm the Commerce Secretary — the one [documented on Epstein's island](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/a-list-of-powerful-men-named-in-the-epstein-files-from-elon-musk-to-former-prince-andrew) contradicting his own timeline, the one who [called Epstein "the greatest blackmailer ever"](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/1/31/bill-gates-elon-musk-howard-lutnick-face-new-scrutiny-over-epstein-ties) while claiming the relationship was over — co-signed that individual's presence inside the government. The question of whether Epstein connections were raised during confirmation hearings, and whether the senators who voted yes knew what they were signing, is a question the record will eventually answer.

And the speed matters. The pace of appointments — the rush to install politicals across every agency, as fast as the Senate can process them — is itself a co-signing strategy. Every confirmed appointment is another name on the paper. Every installation deepens the party's investment in the arrangement. And every political, once installed, extends the co-sign into the operational layer of the government — setting direction for career staff who execute without necessarily understanding what they are protecting.

The confirmation process is not incidental to the co-sign. It *is* the co-sign — the mechanism by which a political party formally attaches its institutional authority to the individuals executing the arrangement described in this series. And it is a mechanism that, once engaged, cannot be reversed without the political cost of admitting what was co-signed in the first place.

## III. The enforcement

A co-sign holds because both parties understand what happens if it doesn't. In finance, the enforcement mechanism is the contract — the legal obligation that makes default costly. In a political party, the enforcement mechanism is the primary.

This is not a metaphor. It is the documented, operational system by which the party ensures that co-signers do not attempt to remove their names from the paper.

### The cost function

The dataset from Section II tells the story: eight out of ten members who broke ranks on the second impeachment were destroyed or driven out. The third-ranking House member went from leadership to losing her primary by 37 points. The party did not merely disagree with defectors. It recruited, funded, and promoted their replacements — systematically, publicly, and as a warning. Every member of the caucus who considers breaking ranks on any element of the arrangement documented in this series makes their calculation against this backdrop.

### The levers

The primary is the blunt instrument. But the party's enforcement architecture is more granular. Committee assignments, fundraising access, and leadership inclusion can all be redirected away from members who break ranks and toward their primary challengers in a single cycle. The impeachment defectors saw every lever activated simultaneously.

The most important lever may be the least visible: **the information asymmetry.** Leadership has access to information that rank-and-file members do not. If the speaker, the majority leader, or the attorney general knows whose names appear in the unreleased 37 pages — and has communicated that knowledge, even obliquely, to the individuals involved — then the enforcement is not a threat delivered from above. It is a shared understanding of mutual exposure. The co-signers at the top know what the co-signers below them have to lose. The co-signers below know that the co-signers above them know. Nothing needs to be said aloud.

This is the same structural logic from Part I: "The incentives are the coordination mechanism." But here the incentives are not between the debtor and his foreign creditors. They are between the debtor and his own party — the people who signed the paper, who hold the levers, and who understand that the only thing worse than protecting the arrangement is being the person who causes it to collapse.

## IV. The current posture

This is not history. The co-signers are signing right now. Every day that the 37 pages remain missing, every hearing that is not convened, every subpoena that is not issued — each is a fresh signature on the paper. And the obligations being co-signed are escalating.

### The file suppression

The DOJ withheld FBI interviews naming the president. It blamed a coding error. It released 16 pages after NPR's reporting. It has not released the remaining 37. Both chairs of the House Oversight Committee [opened investigations](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/25/nx-s1-5726780/democrats-doj-epstein-files-trump-missing-pages).

The "coding error" explanation deserves more scrutiny than it has received — because it is not merely suspicious. To anyone familiar with how federal records management actually operates, it is technically implausible.

The word "coding" in this context does not refer to software. It refers to the categorization and tagging process by which human reviewers classify documents for release, redaction, or withholding during a mandated disclosure. A document release of this kind — compelled by a near-unanimous act of Congress — does not work by pressing a button and hoping the database is correct. It works by reviewers examining documents, assigning them categories, and making affirmative decisions about what to include. The "error" the DOJ described is not a software glitch. It is a claim that human reviewers, during an affirmative review process, tagged these specific documents — FBI interviews naming the president — as "duplicative" and excluded them from a release mandated by law.

Federal agencies do not maintain a single copy of a document in a single system with a single classification tag. They operate under the [Federal Records Act](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R43072) (44 U.S.C. Chapters 29, 31, and 33) and NARA regulations ([36 CFR Chapter XII](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-36/chapter-XII/subchapter-B/part-1220)) that require agencies to "create and maintain authentic, reliable, and usable records and ensure that they remain so for the length of their authorized retention period." Agencies must integrate records management into every electronic information system, maintain chain-of-custody documentation, and [develop records schedules approved by NARA](https://www.ed.gov/about/ed-overview/required-notices/federal-records-act) before any records can be destroyed or reclassified. Federal records [may not be destroyed, mutilated, or removed from government custody without authorization](https://policymanual.nih.gov/1743). A document does not get tagged as "duplicative" and excluded from a congressionally mandated release — not without that categorization surviving quality assurance checks and going undetected by the career staff responsible for compliance. That is not an error in tagging. That is a process — one that requires either a systemic failure across every safeguard the federal records infrastructure is designed to provide, or a deliberate decision implemented through that infrastructure.

The framing matters. "Coding error" implies a keystroke, a glitch, an accident — the language of software, not of human review. But this was a human review process. The question is not whether a database malfunctioned. The question is who reviewed these documents, who categorized them as duplicative, under whose guidance, and why the resulting exclusion — which uniformly affected FBI interviews naming the president — survived every layer of quality assurance in a release that Congress had mandated by a vote of 427 to 1.

*"We know they are lying, they know they are lying, they know we know they are lying, we know they know we know they are lying, but they are still lying."*

Solzhenitsyn wrote that about the Soviet system. The structural parallel is not the ideology. It is the mechanics — the process by which an explanation that no one believes is offered, accepted, and allowed to stand, because every party with the power to challenge it has calculated that the cost of challenging it is higher than the cost of accepting it. The "coding error" is not meant to be believed. It is meant to be *sufficient* — sufficient to fill the space where an explanation is required, so that the committees can note it, the press can report it, and the system can move on without anyone having to say aloud what actually happened.

The gap between announcement and action is the co-sign in its purest form. Both chairs opened investigations. As of this writing, no emergency hearings have been convened on the missing 37 pages. No subpoenas have been issued to compel the DOJ to produce the withheld records. No demand has been made for a technical audit of the records management process that produced the "coding error." The committees that hold the power to force compliance have not used it.

The Judiciary Committee has primary oversight of the Department of Justice. Its jurisdiction includes the conduct of the attorney general, the handling of federal investigations, and the integrity of the file release process. It has not convened a hearing on the documented suppression.

### The war

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel [launched Operation Epic Fury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Strait_of_Hormuz_crisis) — coordinated strikes on Iranian military and nuclear facilities. Congress was not asked to authorize the operation. The War Powers Act requires the president to notify Congress within 48 hours and limits unauthorized hostilities to 60 days without explicit legislative approval. [Lawfare concluded](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/operation-epic-fury-puts-congress-and-the-constitution-to-the-test) that "no such interpretation under Article I and the WPR is plausible with the current intervention in Iran" and that "the scale, scope, and objectives of the campaign indicate that this is a war, not a limited military engagement."

A bipartisan War Powers resolution, led by Massie and Khanna in the House and Kaine and Paul in the Senate, was brought to a vote. The [Senate blocked it 47–53](https://breakingdefense.com/2026/03/iran-war-powers-resolution-fails-in-senate/), with only one member of the majority — Rand Paul — voting in favor. The [House defeated it 212–219](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/house-votes-let-trumps-operation-epic-fury-continue-iran), with near-unified majority opposition. The majority whip [called the operation](https://www.dailysignal.com/2026/03/02/congress-vote-operation-epic-fury-war-powers/) "a bold, decisive act of strength by President Trump." Massie, the co-sponsor of the resolution, [said on the House floor](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/house-votes-let-trumps-operation-epic-fury-continue-iran): "If Congress wants war, then the speaker should hold a vote to declare it." The speaker did not hold a vote to declare it. The war continued. The co-sign held.

### The sanctions relief

On March 12, 2026, the Treasury [lifted sanctions on Russian oil at sea](https://thehill.com/policy/energy-environment/5782247-trump-lifts-russian-oil-sanctions/) — six days after [multiple outlets confirmed](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/03/06/russia-iran-intelligence-us-targets/) that Russia was providing Iran with targeting intelligence used to locate and kill American service members. Six Americans are dead.

[Statements — or silence — from members of the majority on the sanctions relief tell the story in two registers. A handful spoke. Rep. Don Bacon of Nebraska [posted on X](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/12/ukraine-supporters-oppose-easing-of-russia-sanctions/): "Reducing sanctions on Putin's Russia when we just learned the Russians are giving targeting intelligence to Iran is clearly the WRONG strategy. The Russians are helping Iran to kill our service members. We want more sanctions on Russia, not less." Sen. Roger Wicker of Mississippi [said](https://www.newstribune.com/news/2026/mar/13/numerous-lawmakers-outraged-about-easing-of/): "I deeply regret that we are apparently reducing sanctions on Russian oil, because there are credible reports they're aiding Iran in killing our servicemen and women." Rep. Michael McCaul of Texas [posted](https://www.newsweek.com/trump-dismisses-russia-giving-iran-intel-on-us-troops-11640713): "Putin is reportedly returning the favor by providing Iran with intelligence meant to help target and kill Americans." But apart from these exceptions, [several Senate Republicans, when asked about the issue, declined to address it](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/12/ukraine-supporters-oppose-easing-of-russia-sanctions/). The silence from the rest of the caucus — on a matter of American troops being killed with Russian assistance — is the co-sign made audible.]

Senator Elissa Slotkin, a former CIA and Pentagon official, [said](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/team-trump-offers-russia-more-sanctions-relief-brushing-off-allegations-its-aiding-iran): "If Russia is helping to kill U.S. forces, we have crossed a Rubicon." Senator Chris Coons [called Russia](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/team-trump-offers-russia-more-sanctions-relief-brushing-off-allegations-its-aiding-iran) "the clearest winner from this war in Iran."

The asymmetry is documented. Three members of the majority spoke. The rest of the caucus was silent — or, in the case of the president's envoy Steve Witkoff, [appeared on national television](https://www.ms.now/rachel-maddow-show/maddowblog/white-houses-witkoff-vouches-for-russias-trustworthiness-on-iran-and-alleged-war-assistance) to vouch for Russia's trustworthiness, telling CNBC that Russian officials "said they have not been sharing" intelligence with Iran and that "we can take them at their word."

The dissent is real and should not be dismissed. But its contours are informative. Bacon, Wicker, and McCaul are members whose political identities are built on hawkish national-security credentials — in districts and states where military families vote and defense spending is an economic lifeline. For them, silence on Russian intelligence-sharing that killed American troops would be more costly than speech. Their objections come from positions where speaking out serves their electoral interests. The question is not whether they are sincere. The question is why their objections stopped at statements. None of the three introduced legislation to block the sanctions relief. None demanded a hearing. None issued a subpoena. They spoke — and then the caucus moved on. The dissent was absorbed, not acted upon. And the difference between a statement and a subpoena is the distance between the appearance of opposition and the exercise of it.

### The midterms

In January 2026, the president [floated canceling the midterm elections](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/17/politics/midterm-elections-trump-2026-analysis). The White House said he was "speaking facetiously." In February, the [*Washington Post* reported](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2026/02/26/trump-elections-executive-order-activists/) that a 17-page draft executive order was circulating among activists coordinating with the White House, claiming Chinese election interference as a basis to declare a national emergency and seize control of voting mechanisms. No leader of the majority has stated unequivocally and on the record that the midterm elections must proceed as scheduled, without interference, under existing state administration. The absence of that statement — which should be the easiest sentence in American politics to say — is itself a signature on the paper.

This is the co-sign that completes the circle. Part I established that the midterms are the deadline — the point at which oversight begins, subpoena power shifts, and every domain of exposure becomes subject to investigation by a body the president does not control. The co-signers have a stake in that deadline not arriving for the same reason the debtor does: oversight does not discriminate between the borrower and the guarantor. If the files come out, if the financial arrangements are investigated, if the Iran terms are exposed — the names on the paper include theirs.

## V. The operational layer

Political analysis typically stops at the elected official. It should not. The co-sign extends deeper than the people who cast votes on C-SPAN.

Below the secretaries and the undersecretaries and the deputy assistants — below every confirmed appointee and every political hire — is the federal workforce. Career employees who process FOIA requests, manage classified document releases, execute sanctions policy, and administer elections. They do not set direction. They follow it. And the direction is set by the politicals above them.

This is not a flaw in the system. It is the system. Political appointees exist to translate the president's agenda into operational reality. Career staff exist to execute. The structure is designed so that elections have consequences — so that when the public chooses a president, the government's direction changes accordingly. It is, under normal conditions, a feature.

Under the conditions described in this series, it is the mechanism by which a co-sign reaches into every agency in the federal government. The attorney general does not personally redact documents. A career employee does — following guidance established by the political layer. The Treasury Secretary does not personally draft sanctions waivers. Career staff do — implementing the policy direction set by politicals. The "coding error" that suppressed 50 pages of FBI interviews was not executed by the attorney general. It was executed by the system she directs. And every senator who voted to confirm her co-signed the system's output.

The tension between career staff obligations and political direction is where the co-sign becomes operationally visible — but it is also, by design, the layer that is hardest to document. Career staff who object do not hold press conferences. They file internal complaints, contact inspectors general, or leave. Whether any have done so in connection with the file suppression or the sanctions relief is not yet part of the public record. But the structure ensures that the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — it is evidence of a system working as the politicals intend.

The speed of appointments matters here. The more politicals installed, the deeper the direction penetrates. The fewer career officials left in acting or senior roles, the less institutional resistance exists between the political directive and the operational outcome. Every appointment is a layer of insulation between the co-signers at the top and the actions being taken on their behalf at the bottom. And every confirmation — every Senate vote to install another political — is another signature on the paper.

## VI. The question

This series has asked, at the end of each installment, what the reader must accept in order to believe the conventional explanation. This piece asks a different version of the same question.

The conventional explanation for the majority's behavior requires the reader to accept:

That a Congress which voted 427–1 for transparency reversed its posture for reasons unrelated to the contents of the files it voted to release.

That the systematic suppression of documents by the DOJ is a bureaucratic error that the oversight committees have no institutional interest in correcting.

That the silence on Russian intelligence-sharing — which resulted in the deaths of six American service members — and the subsequent reward of sanctions relief reflects a legitimate policy disagreement rather than a co-signer protecting the arrangement that produced it.

That a Congress which built its modern identity on military strength, opposition to Russian aggression, and suspicion of foreign entanglement has no concerns about a war launched without authorization that simultaneously served the strategic objectives of two foreign governments documented in Part I as holding leverage over the president.

That the pace of political appointments is routine rather than structural — unrelated to the need to ensure that every agency involved in the arrangement is directed by people whose positions depend on the arrangement surviving.

That none of the individuals participating in the protection of this arrangement have personal exposure that explains their behavior.

The structural explanation requires only one assumption: that a co-signer protects the debtor because the debtor's default is the co-signer's default. The ratchet turned for six years. Every signature made the next one cheaper and the cost of reversal higher. By the time the files started naming names, the co-signers had already signed so many times that defection was indistinguishable from self-destruction. And the behavior that results — the suppression, the silence, the active protection of an arrangement that serves foreign creditors at the expense of American lives — is not loyalty. It is not ideology. It is not even cowardice.

It is the arithmetic of a co-sign. The borrower defaults, and you pay. So you make sure the borrower never defaults. Whatever it costs. Whoever it kills.
