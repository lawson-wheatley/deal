## The hedge

In finance, a hedge is a position taken to protect against the possibility that your primary position fails. It does not generate returns on its own. It exists to ensure that even if conditions turn against you, the downside is contained. A good hedge does not need to succeed. It needs to prevent failure.

Parts I through IV of this series documented a closing in progress — debts being settled in policy, in sanctions relief, in American blood — the apparatus that produced the leverage, the party that co-signed the debtor's obligations, and the counterparties on both sides whose structural constraints prevent them from unwinding the deal. The natural question, after four installments, is: what stops it?

The answer is oversight. Congressional subpoena power. Inspectors general. Career officials who know where the records are. An independent judiciary. A Department of Justice that can investigate without political direction. Elections that bring new majorities with new investigative authority. Every one of these mechanisms is, as this piece is being written, under systematic assault.

The public sees chaos. This series sees a hedge — the active protection of a closing in progress, executed through the systematic degradation of every institutional mechanism that could unwind it. Project 2025 is not an ideology. It is the operational manual for foreclosing accountability within a finite window.

## I. The precedent

The sequence is not new. It has played out in enough countries, across enough decades, that political scientists have given it a name: democratic backsliding. But that term is clinical. The structural pattern is simpler. A leader with exposure — criminal, financial, or both — arrives in office and immediately begins dismantling the institutional machinery that could hold them accountable. Not after the investigation begins. Before it can.

The names the reader already knows established the template. Hitler used the Reichstag Fire Decree and the Enabling Act to suspend civil liberties and bypass the legislature within two months of taking power. The judiciary was restructured. The civil service was purged under the Law for the Restoration of the Professional Civil Service, which removed officials deemed politically unreliable and replaced them with party loyalists. The independent press was eliminated. By the time the full scope of the regime's actions became apparent, no domestic institution existed to check them. Stalin's consolidation followed the same structural logic — purges of the party, the military, the judiciary, and the intelligentsia that eliminated every internal mechanism of accountability before the machinery of repression was fully operational.

These are the extreme cases. The reader may believe they are distant from the current moment. They are not the point. The point is the pattern — and it operates on a spectrum. Mussolini captured the Italian state more gradually, using legal mechanisms and coalition politics to hollow out oversight before dispensing with it. Orbán has spent over a decade in Hungary rewriting the constitution, packing the courts, restructuring election law, and capturing media — not through violence but through legislation, appointment, and procedural manipulation. Each step was legal. The cumulative effect was the elimination of meaningful accountability.

The modern case the reader is least likely to know is the most instructive. In the Philippines, Duterte [used the Constitution to dismantle checks and balances](https://www.thenation.com/article/world/rodrigo-duterte-philippines/) while enjoying approval ratings above 70%. He packed the Supreme Court — [handpicking 13 of 15 justices](https://www.asiamaior.org/the-journal/13-asia-maior-vol-xxx-2019/the-philippines-2018-2019-authoritarian-consolidation-under-duterte.html) by the time he left office — gutted congressional oversight by filling both chambers with allies, shuttered the country's largest independent media network, and [fast-tracked legislation that institutionalized emergency powers](https://www.fletcherforum.org/home/6/18/2025/beyond-the-international-criminal-court-dutertes-pandemic-playbook) without sunset clauses. When the ICC eventually charged him with crimes against humanity, the institutional machinery that might have held him accountable domestically had already been hollowed out. He was [arrested and sent to The Hague](https://www.thenation.com/article/world/rodrigo-duterte-philippines/) — but only because an international court, outside the system he had captured, still functioned.

The same pattern is visible in real time. Putin dismantled Russia's independent judiciary, subordinated its media, and converted the Duma into a rubber stamp within his first two terms. The ICC [issued an arrest warrant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court_arrest_warrants_for_Israeli_leaders) in 2023 for war crimes in Ukraine. He has not been arrested. He simply does not travel to countries that would enforce it. Netanyahu — facing [domestic criminal prosecution on corruption charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court_arrest_warrants_for_Israeli_leaders) — prosecuted a war that consolidated his coalition and made his removal politically untenable. The ICC [issued warrants for war crimes and crimes against humanity in November 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court_arrest_warrants_for_Israeli_leaders). He cannot travel to 125 member states. He remains in power. In both cases, the domestic institutions that might have acted were captured first. The ICC is the only remaining backstop — the one institution outside the system that can act when every institution inside it has been neutralized.

That backstop does not exist for the United States. The [American Service-Members' Protection Act of 2002](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Service-Members%27_Protection_Act) — colloquially known as the Hague Invasion Act — authorizes the president to use ["all means necessary and appropriate"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Service-Members%27_Protection_Act) to liberate any American or allied person detained by the ICC. The law extends this protection to officials of NATO members and major non-NATO allies [including Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Service-Members%27_Protection_Act) — which is why the United States shields Netanyahu from ICC enforcement even as it supported the same court's warrant against Putin. The law prohibits federal, state, and local governments from assisting the ICC. It authorizes, in plain statutory language, military force against the court itself.

The sequence is always the same: capture the legislature, capture the judiciary, replace the civil service, weaponize the justice system, degrade the electoral machinery. The United States has pre-emptively closed the one door that caught Duterte, holds it closed for its allies, and is now executing the rest of the sequence with better infrastructure than any predecessor. No previous government attempting this had a 900-page operational manual, a pre-vetted personnel database of 20,000 loyalists, a 180-day implementation playbook, and the explicit institutional support of more than 100 organizations. The United States has one.

## II. The blueprint

Project 2025 — formally titled *Mandate for Leadership: The Conservative Promise* — was [published in April 2023](https://www.heritage.org/press/project-2025-publishes-comprehensive-policy-guide-mandate-leadership-the-conservative-promise) by the Heritage Foundation and more than 100 affiliated conservative organizations. Its stated goal is the restructuring of the federal government to consolidate executive power. It is a 900-page document with 30 chapters spanning every federal department, authored by more than 350 contributors, [most of whom worked in the first Trump administration or his 2024 campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_2025). It includes not just policy proposals but a personnel database, a training academy, and a 180-day implementation playbook — what Heritage describes as a plan to ensure the next conservative president can begin executing on "Day One."

Trump publicly distanced himself from the project during the campaign. After winning the 2024 election, he [nominated several of its architects and supporters to senior positions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_2025). Within four days of his inauguration, Time magazine found that [nearly two-thirds of his executive actions mirrored or partially mirrored Project 2025 proposals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_2025). Heritage Foundation president Kevin Roberts [described the organization's role](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_2025) as "institutionalizing Trumpism." Russell Vought, who [authored Project 2025's chapter on the Executive Office of the President](https://www.aclu.org/project-2025-explained) and reportedly spearheaded its 180-day implementation playbook, was confirmed as director of the Office of Management and Budget — the office that controls the enforcement of presidential policy across the entire federal government.

The document's own language is instructive. Its stated goal is to ["assemble an army of aligned, vetted, trained, and prepared conservatives to go to work on Day One to deconstruct the Administrative State."](https://static.heritage.org/project2025/2025_MandateForLeadership_FULL.pdf) The Administrative State is the federal workforce. To deconstruct it is to remove the people who perform oversight functions. Every element maps to a specific accountability mechanism. What follows is the mapping.

## III. The mechanisms

### Schedule F: Converting the workforce

The central workforce provision of Project 2025 is the reclassification of career civil servants into a new category of at-will employees. Originally created by [Executive Order 13957 on October 21, 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schedule_F_appointment), during Trump's first term, the initiative was conceived by domestic policy adviser James Sherk, who [found a provision in Title 5 of the U.S. Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schedule_F_appointment) that could be used to strip civil service protections from employees in "policy-influencing" positions. Biden revoked the order on his first day in office. Trump [reinstated it on his first day back](https://www.epi.org/policywatch/eo-restoring-accountability-to-policy-influencing-positions-within-the-federal-workforce/), January 20, 2025.

The reclassified designation — now renamed "Schedule Policy/Career" — was [finalized by the Office of Personnel Management on February 5, 2026](https://www.newsnationnow.com/politics/project-2025-federal-workers-takes-effect/). It [targets approximately 50,000 career federal employees](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-creates-new-federal-employee-category-to-enhance-accountability/), roughly 2% of the workforce, converting them to at-will status. The rule allows agencies to remove employees for "misconduct, poor performance, or deliberate obstruction of presidential policy" — language broad enough to encompass any career official who raises concerns about the legality of a directive.

Project 2025 states explicitly that the president ["must recognize that whoever holds a government position sets its policy"](https://www.newsnationnow.com/politics/project-2025-federal-workers-takes-effect/) and must therefore give personnel management his "highest priority." The provision does not reduce the number of positions. It [allows the administration to fill reclassified roles directly with political appointees](https://www.appliedpolicy.com/federal-workforce-reform-revisiting-schedule-f/). Scholar Don Moynihan called Schedule F ["the most profound change to the civil service system since its creation in 1883."](https://kettering.org/the-schedule-f-threat-to-democracy-a-project-2025-explainer/)

The operational layer this neutralizes was documented in Part III, Section V. Replace the career staff who process FOIA requests, manage classified document releases, and execute sanctions policy with political appointees — and the "coding error" from Part I becomes the operating model.

OPM received [40,500 public comments on the proposed rule — 94% opposed](https://www.govexec.com/workforce/2025/11/final-schedule-f-regulations-describe-civil-service-protections-unconstitutional-overcorrections/409616/). Federal employee unions [filed lawsuits on the first day after reinstatement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schedule_F_appointment). The rule was finalized over their objections.

### Inspector general removals: Eliminating the watchdogs

On the night of January 24, 2025 — the fifth day of the administration — Trump [fired at least 17 inspectors general](https://www.npr.org/2025/01/25/g-s1-44771/trump-fires-inspectors-general) across federal agencies, effective immediately. The firings were announced via email, citing "changing priorities." Congress was not given the [30-day notice and substantive rationale required by the 1978 Inspector General Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_dismissals_of_U.S._inspectors_general). Among those fired were the inspectors general of the [Department of Defense, State Department, Department of Veterans Affairs, Department of Energy, and Department of Transportation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_dismissals_of_U.S._inspectors_general).

The legal assessment is unambiguous. On September 24, 2025, federal judge Ana C. Reyes [ruled that Trump violated the Inspector General Act](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/agency-oversight/2025/09/trump-unlawfully-fired-17-agency-igs-judge-finds-but-wont-reinstate-them/) — a finding she described as "obvious" — but declined to reinstate the inspectors general, reasoning that Trump could simply re-fire them after providing the legally required notice. By October 2025, [over 75% of presidentially appointed inspector general positions were vacant](https://ourpublicservice.org/blog/president-trumps-firing-of-inspectors-general-threatens-government-accountability-and-efficiency/). The White House subsequently [blocked funds appropriated by Congress to the Council of the Inspectors General on Integrity and Efficiency](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system), the umbrella entity that coordinates the inspector general community.

Several of the fired inspectors general had been [investigating matters related to Trump's political allies](https://americanoversight.org/investigation/trumps-illegal-firing-of-inspectors-general/). The Defense Department inspector general, Robert Storch, had in late 2024 opened a review of SpaceX's compliance with federal reporting protocols intended to protect state secrets. The Agriculture Department's Phyllis Fong had been investigating Elon Musk's Neuralink startup. The Labor and Transportation Department inspectors general led agencies that had previously investigated or fined Musk's companies.

Remove the inspectors general and the only path for a whistleblower is the press — risky, slow, and easily discredited. Or silence. Storch told [PBS](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/inspectors-general-fired-by-trump-issue-warning-about-lack-of-oversight) the mass firing sends a message that "there's not receptiveness to the sort of oversight" the watchdogs are tasked with providing.

### DOJ centralization: Capturing the prosecution function

The Department of Justice has been restructured to centralize authority under political appointees to a degree that former officials describe as unprecedented.

Within one week of the inauguration, the department's senior leadership [issued a memo redelegating approval authority on ethics and other sensitive decisions](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/us-law-week/how-trumps-justice-department-departed-from-tradition-in-2025) to politically appointed aides — the first time in modern history that a career civil servant was removed from the final judgment on employee discipline, recusals, and congressional disclosures. The department subsequently [removed the head of the Office of Professional Responsibility](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/us-law-week/how-trumps-justice-department-departed-from-tradition-in-2025), which handles attorney misconduct investigations, and [fired the ethics director](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system) who advised the attorney general and senior leaders. Both positions remained vacant through the end of 2025. The department also [gutted the Public Integrity Section of the Criminal Division](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system), an office created in response to the Watergate scandal that has combated public corruption for nearly 50 years.

Attorney General Pam Bondi [issued a policy on her first day](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system) declaring that any DOJ attorney who refused to sign a brief or appear in court to defend the administration's actions would be subject to discipline. A former DOJ official told NPR that the White House is now [directing the positions the department takes in court and shaping the language of its legal filings](https://www.npr.org/2025/05/06/g-s1-64305/weaponization-doj-trump-bondi-justice-department).

The practical consequences are visible. When career prosecutors in New York refused to drop the corruption case against New York City Mayor Eric Adams — a case Bondi directed them to dismiss, [arguing it interfered with the mayor's ability to help Trump with immigration enforcement](https://www.npr.org/2025/05/06/g-s1-64305/weaponization-doj-trump-bondi-justice-department) — nearly a dozen prosecutors resigned in protest. The Public Integrity Section, the Civil Rights Division, and the Federal Programs Branch have each [lost a majority of their attorneys](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system) since January 2025. The department also [demoted or fired dozens of career employees](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system) perceived to be insufficiently loyal or who had previously worked on prosecutions the president opposed.

Former pardon attorney Liz Oyer, who was [fired after declining to restore gun rights to actor Mel Gibson](https://www.npr.org/2025/05/06/g-s1-64305/weaponization-doj-trump-bondi-justice-department), told NPR: "The Department of Justice is essentially whatever the president wants it to be right now."

The department also established an unprecedented ["Weaponization Working Group"](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/project-2025s-plan-criminal-justice-under-trump) whose leader [promised to charge the president's political enemies](https://www.ibanet.org/US-presidency-weaponised-Department-of-Justice-investigations-prompt-concerns-over-independence). Trump has [publicly directed the DOJ to investigate](https://www.npr.org/2025/09/22/nx-s1-5550132/trump-justice-department-comey-letitia-james-virginia) former FBI Director James Comey, New York Attorney General Letitia James, and Senator Adam Schiff — all of whom had previously investigated him. The administration [reversed the post-Watergate policy](https://protectdemocracy.org/work/assessing-trump-dojs-investigations-prosecutions/) that limited White House contact with the DOJ on ongoing cases. Former White House ethics counsel Richard Painter told the [International Bar Association](https://www.ibanet.org/US-presidency-weaponised-Department-of-Justice-investigations-prompt-concerns-over-independence) the pattern represents "a direct assault on the non-political administration of justice."

The DOJ is the entity that would investigate and prosecute the arrangements documented in this series. Under its current leadership, it is an instrument of them.

### DOGE: Destroying institutional memory

The Department of Government Efficiency — a rebrand of the existing U.S. Digital Service, repurposed to oversee broad reductions in federal employment — was the operational vehicle for the largest workforce cuts. Its stated purpose is efficiency. Its structural effect is the removal of institutional capacity.

The scale is documented. According to the Office of Personnel Management, [approximately 317,000 federal employees left their jobs in 2025](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-10/federal-workforce-s-toll-after-a-year-of-doge-and-trump-317-000). Of those, roughly 24,000 were involuntary separations, including 17,000 through formal reductions in force. Another 154,000 signed up for the "deferred resignation" program. Brookings documented [25,747 instances where the administration fired people and then rehired them](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/how-many-people-can-the-federal-government-lose-before-it-crashes/) — a pattern of chaos that is itself functionally destructive even when the firings are reversed, because it disrupts institutional continuity and drives voluntary departures.

Specific impacts are measurable. According to Science Magazine, roughly [10,109 STEM experts left federal service in 2025](https://www.allsides.com/blog/doge-effect-tracking-trumps-cuts-federal-agencies-and-funding), representing 14% of the total number of Ph.D.s employed at the end of 2024. The Agriculture Department [lost more than 20,000 employees between January and June](https://www.allsides.com/blog/doge-effect-tracking-trumps-cuts-federal-agencies-and-funding). The IRS cuts alone are estimated to result in [$2.4 trillion in lost revenue over 10 years](https://democrats-budget.house.gov/resources/report/doges-mass-firings-result-gutted-services-and-higher-costs) due to reduced enforcement — a Yale Budget Lab finding that means the "efficiency" operation is projected to cost the treasury more than it saves by several orders of magnitude.

The legal scholar Donald Moynihan, in a peer-reviewed journal article, described the mass firings as an ["anti-statist restructuring of American government centered around political loyalty."](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_United_States_federal_mass_layoffs)

On July 8, 2025, the Supreme Court [overrode lower court orders that had frozen the workforce reductions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_United_States_federal_mass_layoffs), allowing them to continue.

But the DOGE operation was not limited to removing people. It also involved accessing systems. DOGE operatives [gained entry to Treasury Department payment systems](https://www.cbpp.org/research/federal-budget/doge-access-to-treasury-payment-systems-raises-serious-risks) that process trillions of dollars in government transactions and contain Social Security numbers, bank account information, and tax data for millions of Americans — systems previously managed exclusively by career civil servants with extensive security screening. NPR reported that DOGE staffers [skirted privacy laws, training, and security protocols](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/26/nx-s1-5339842/doge-data-access-privacy-act-social-security-treasury-opm-lawsuit) to gain access to databases across at least seven major agencies including OPM, Treasury, and the Departments of Education, Energy, and Health and Human Services. A federal judge found the access was ["rushed and undertaken by political pressure"](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/26/nx-s1-5339842/doge-data-access-privacy-act-social-security-treasury-opm-lawsuit) and warned that "a real possibility exists that sensitive information has already been shared outside of the Treasury Department, in potential violation of federal law." One DOGE employee was found to have [sent a spreadsheet containing personally identifiable information to officials at another agency](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/26/nx-s1-5339842/doge-data-access-privacy-act-social-security-treasury-opm-lawsuit).

In June 2025, the Washington Post reported that DOGE personnel had [installed a Starlink terminal on a building adjacent to the White House](https://foreignpolicy.com/2025/06/12/elon-musk-trump-putin-security-risk-starlink-spacex-dhs-justice-department/), bypassing the security restrictions normally imposed on White House communications. Those managing the systems were unable to monitor the connection or stop sensitive information from leaving the complex. Three days later, the Wall Street Journal reported that [DHS and the DOJ had tracked foreign nationals — including people from Eastern Europe — visiting Musk's properties](https://foreignpolicy.com/2025/06/12/elon-musk-trump-putin-security-risk-starlink-spacex-dhs-justice-department/) in 2022 and 2023. This is the same individual who was reported in 2024 to have been in [regular phone contact with Russian President Vladimir Putin since late 2022](https://foreignpolicy.com/2025/06/12/elon-musk-trump-putin-security-risk-starlink-spacex-dhs-justice-department/) — conversations in which Putin asked Musk, among other things, to refrain from activating Starlink over Taiwan as a favor to Xi Jinping. The same individual whose companies hold [a $1.8 billion classified contract with the National Reconnaissance Office](https://foreignpolicy.com/2025/06/12/elon-musk-trump-putin-security-risk-starlink-spacex-dhs-justice-department/) to build spy satellite networks — and whose Starlink terminals were [found to be in use by Russian forces in occupied Ukraine](https://www.defenseone.com/threats/2024/02/russia-using-spacexs-starlink-satellite-devices-ukraine-sources-say/394080/), guiding attack drones against the country they were originally deployed to help.

The DOGE operatives' mandate [expires July 4, 2026](https://www.nextgov.com/people/2026/01/us-doge-service-hiring-following-mass-workforce-losses-across-government/410496/) — the 250th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence. The semiquincentennial. The administration that frames its project as completing a founding revolution set the expiration date for its restructuring operation on the day the country turns 250. The operatives leave. The damage stays. Four months before the midterms, the institutional capacity has already been removed.

You cannot investigate what has already been transferred out of a building that no longer exists.

### Judicial capture: Closing the courtroom

Trump's first term produced [234 confirmed Article III judges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_federal_judges_appointed_by_Donald_Trump), including three Supreme Court justices and 54 appellate judges — a reshaping of the federal bench that commentators and advocacy groups treated as a signature achievement. His cumulative total across both terms reached [267 confirmed Article III judges as of March 2026](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_federal_judges_appointed_by_Donald_Trump).

The second term's confirmation pace has been slower — [26 judges confirmed in 2025](https://rollcall.com/2025/12/30/trumps-2025-saw-26-lifetime-judicial-nominees-approved/), behind Biden's first-year pace — in part because [Biden's 189 district court appointments left fewer vacancies](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/paucity-of-vacancies-slows-trumps-effort-to-reshape-courts/) and because Democratic appointees have been [reluctant to create new vacancies by retiring](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/paucity-of-vacancies-slows-trumps-effort-to-reshape-courts/). But the structural impact of the first-term appointments endures. The first term flipped several circuits from Democratic-appointee majorities to Republican-appointee majorities, and those judges serve lifetime terms.

Bloomberg Law reported that [this year's nominees skewed even younger than first-term picks](https://news.bgov.com/us-law-week/trump-judicial-appointments-slow-as-vacancies-scarce-for-2026), with roughly half of the 33 nominees who had confirmation hearings being 44 or younger. The Alliance for Justice noted that nominees appear to have been [vetted for loyalty](https://rollcall.com/2025/12/30/trumps-2025-saw-26-lifetime-judicial-nominees-approved/) to a degree that may explain the slower pace.

The judiciary hears challenges to every other element of the hedge — Schedule F, the inspector general firings, the DOJ restructuring, the election orders. The more of those judges confirmed through the co-signing process documented in Part III, the less likely those challenges are to succeed.

### Press capture: Closing the fourth estate

The press broke the stories this series relies on. NPR [revealed the systematic suppression](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/24/nx-s1-5723968/epstein-files-trump-accusation-maxwell) of FBI interviews naming the president. The Washington Post [confirmed the Russian intelligence-sharing](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/03/06/russia-iran-intelligence-us-targets/) that contributed to the deaths of American service members. CNN [verified the Epstein file contents](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/24/us/epstein-files-trump-accuser-missing-files-invs). PBS [reviewed the draft election executive order](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/trump-says-hes-not-mulling-a-draft-executive-order-to-seize-control-over-elections-heres-what-we-know). If those outlets had not done that work, most of the documented facts in this series would not be public. The fourth estate is the mechanism that made this series possible — and it is being degraded on the same timeline as every other oversight function.

The instrument is not censorship. It is the dependency itself — the same structural logic that governs every other capture mechanism documented in this series. Broadcasters require FCC licenses and regulatory approval for mergers. The administration has converted that dependency into an editorial enforcement mechanism.

FCC Chairman Brendan Carr — who [authored Project 2025's chapter on the FCC](https://time.com/7312097/trump-abc-nbc-media-threats/) — has [launched formal investigations into every major broadcast network](https://www.npr.org/2025/08/25/nx-s1-5515480/fcc-abc-nbc-reviews): ABC, CBS, NBC, NPR, and PBS. The one network that has escaped scrutiny is Fox, owned by the president's political ally. Carr [reinstated complaints](https://pressfreedomtracker.us/all-incidents/brendan-carr-targets-news-outlets-as-chair-of-the-fcc/) against news outlets that had been dismissed by his predecessor, opened investigations into networks' diversity programs, and [publicly threatened broadcast licenses](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/fcc-brendan-carr-threat-news-networks-broadcast-license/) over coverage he characterized as "news distortion" — a term he has applied to reporting on the Iran war, immigration enforcement, and the wrongful deportation of a Maryland resident.

The enforcement mechanism works through the merger approval process. Paramount needed FCC approval to complete its acquisition by Skydance Media. The FCC had an open investigation into CBS — a Paramount property — over its editing of a *60 Minutes* interview. [Paramount agreed to pay $16 million to settle Trump's lawsuit](https://firstamendment.mtsu.edu/post/abc-cbs-settlements-with-trump-are-dangerous-step-toward-commander-in-chiefs-becoming-editor-in-chief/) against CBS. Days later, Carr met with Skydance's CEO. Days after that, the FCC approved the merger. NPR [reported the sequence directly](https://www.npr.org/2025/08/25/nx-s1-5515480/fcc-abc-nbc-reviews): the lawsuit settled, Carr met with the acquirer, and then — and only then — the merger was approved. Disney had previously settled a separate Trump lawsuit against ABC for [$15 million](https://firstamendment.mtsu.edu/post/abc-cbs-settlements-with-trump-are-dangerous-step-toward-commander-in-chiefs-becoming-editor-in-chief/). CBS anchor John Dickerson [summarized the result](https://firstamendment.mtsu.edu/post/abc-cbs-settlements-with-trump-are-dangerous-step-toward-commander-in-chiefs-becoming-editor-in-chief/) on the evening news: "Can you hold power to account when you've paid it millions?"

The editorial consequences followed. Skydance's new CEO — the son of Oracle founder and Trump ally Larry Ellison — [appointed Bari Weiss as editor-in-chief of CBS](https://time.com/7332902/bbc-controversy-trump-jan-6-documentary-panorama-media-lawsuits-explainer/) and oversaw significant layoffs and changes to editorial direction. When FCC Chairman Carr [publicly threatened ABC's license](https://www.cnn.com/2025/09/19/media/trump-carr-fcc-abc-kimmel-license-threat) over comments by late-night host Jimmy Kimmel, the show was [pulled from the air](https://time.com/7332902/bbc-controversy-trump-jan-6-documentary-panorama-media-lawsuits-explainer/) within hours. The president [celebrated](https://time.com/7332902/bbc-controversy-trump-jan-6-documentary-panorama-media-lawsuits-explainer/): "Great News for America." CBS subsequently [canceled The Late Show with Stephen Colbert](https://time.com/7332902/bbc-controversy-trump-jan-6-documentary-panorama-media-lawsuits-explainer/) after Colbert mocked the Paramount settlement — while the company still needed FCC approval for the merger.

The Republican-led Congress [eliminated all federal funding for public media](https://www.npr.org/2025/08/25/nx-s1-5515480/fcc-abc-nbc-reviews), including NPR — the outlet that broke the Epstein file suppression story. The U.S. Agency for Global Media, which operated Voice of America, Radio Free Europe, and Radio Free Asia — reaching [427 million people per week](https://www.poynter.org/reporting-editing/2025/united-states-press-freedom-donald-trump/) in countries without a free press — was gutted. Workers responsible for handling FOIA requests — the mechanism journalists use to access government records — were [laid off](https://rsf.org/en/one-year-trump-s-second-term-increasingly-repressive-us-president-track-join-ranks-world-s-worst). The DOJ [rescinded the policy against subpoenaing journalists](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-resolution/205/text), exposing sources to identification and punishment.

The Committee to Protect Journalists [found](https://cpj.org/2025/04/trumps-first-100-days-portend-long-lasting-damage-to-press-freedom/) that press freedom is "no longer a given in the United States." Reporters Without Borders [dropped the U.S. to 57th](https://www.poynter.org/reporting-editing/2025/united-states-press-freedom-donald-trump/) in its World Press Freedom Index — the lowest ranking since the index began in 2002. RSF [placed Trump on its 2026 Press Freedom Predators list](https://rsf.org/en/one-year-trump-s-second-term-increasingly-repressive-us-president-track-join-ranks-world-s-worst), calling his actions "an all-out war on press freedom." There have been [170 reported assaults on journalists](https://www.milwaukeeindependent.com/newswire/journalists-face-escalating-threats-2026-trump-intensifies-hostility-toward-free-press/) in the United States in 2025, 160 of them by law enforcement.

This is not a media criticism. It is a structural observation. The press is the mechanism by which the public learns what the parties at the closing table are doing. Every other element of the hedge — the inspector general firings, the DOJ capture, the civil service reclassification, the election interference — removes the government's ability to check itself. This section documents the degradation of the public's ability to check the government. The two functions together — internal oversight removed, external oversight degraded — are the hedge in its complete form.

*"A really efficient totalitarian state would be one in which the all-powerful executive of political bosses and their army of managers control a population of slaves who do not have to be coerced, because they love their servitude."*

Huxley wrote that in 1932 about a future that did not yet exist. The mechanism he described was not suppression — it was distraction, pleasure, and the replacement of information with noise. The modern version does not require that the population love its servitude. It requires only that the press, which would inform them of what is being done, is sufficiently threatened, captured, defunded, or overwhelmed that the information arrives too late, too fragmented, or not at all. The hedge does not need to silence the press. It needs to ensure that what the press publishes does not produce the accountability the series has documented as the one force the closing cannot survive.

## IV. The window

The publicly known deadline is the midterms. But a hedge does not wait for a known deadline when the position could deteriorate sooner. Timelines move up when exposure compounds.

On November 3, 2026, all 435 seats in the House of Representatives and 35 Senate seats [will be contested](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_United_States_elections). A new majority would bring subpoena power, investigative authority, and oversight the president does not control. The 37 missing pages would be subpoenaed. The damage assessment would be demanded. The financial disclosures would be compelled.

Every element of the hedge documented in Section II is calibrated to this deadline. Schedule F was [finalized in February 2026](https://www.newsnationnow.com/politics/project-2025-federal-workers-takes-effect/) — nine months before the election. The inspector general positions [remain over 75% vacant](https://ourpublicservice.org/blog/president-trumps-firing-of-inspectors-general-threatens-government-accountability-and-efficiency/) a year after the firings. The DOJ's institutional capacity to conduct independent investigations has been [systematically degraded](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system) throughout 2025. The question is not whether the administration can complete the restructuring. It is whether enough of it can be completed before oversight arrives that the remaining machinery cannot effectively function.

And the administration has signaled, repeatedly, that the deadline itself is a target.

In early January 2026, Trump [floated the idea of canceling the midterm elections](https://time.com/7346834/trump-canceling-midterm-elections-joking-white-house/) — not once, but twice. In a Reuters interview, he argued "we shouldn't even have an election" because of how much the administration had accomplished. The White House [dismissed the remarks as "facetious."](https://time.com/7346834/trump-canceling-midterm-elections-joking-white-house/) He [repeated the suggestion](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/17/politics/midterm-elections-trump-2026-analysis) at a House Republican retreat.

The reader should note the pattern. He disavowed Project 2025 during the campaign. [Two-thirds of his executive actions mirrored it.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_2025) He said he had ["never heard"](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/trump-says-hes-not-mulling-a-draft-executive-order-to-seize-control-over-elections-heres-what-we-know) of the draft election executive order. It was [dated nine months earlier](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/trump-there-will-be-voter-i-d-for-the-midterm-elections-whether-approved-by-congress-or-not/). In July 2020, he [suggested the presidential election should be postponed](https://time.com/7346834/trump-canceling-midterm-elections-joking-white-house/). The public laughed. He did not win. What followed was January 6. The things this administration announces as jokes have a documented tendency to surface later as executive orders — or worse.

In February 2026, it was reported that pro-Trump activists were [circulating a 17-page draft executive order](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/trump-says-hes-not-mulling-a-draft-executive-order-to-seize-control-over-elections-heres-what-we-know) that would declare a national emergency and give the president extraordinary power over the midterm elections — including requiring hand-counting of ballots, voter re-registration with proof of citizenship, and restrictions on mail-in voting. PBS News [reviewed the document in full](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/trump-says-hes-not-mulling-a-draft-executive-order-to-seize-control-over-elections-heres-what-we-know). Trump denied knowledge of it. Democracy Docket [published the draft](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/trump-there-will-be-voter-i-d-for-the-midterm-elections-whether-approved-by-congress-or-not/). It was dated April 12, 2025.

The election executive order issued in March 2025 — the one that attempted to impose proof-of-citizenship requirements on voter registration — has been [permanently blocked by federal courts](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/judge-rules-trump-cant-require-u-s-citizenship-proof-on-federal-voting-form) as an unconstitutional violation of the separation of powers. Judge Colleen Kollar-Kotelly wrote: "Because our Constitution assigns responsibility for election regulation to the States and to Congress, this Court holds that the President lacks the authority to direct such changes." Multiple additional provisions have been [blocked in subsequent rulings](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/judge-blocks-additional-citizenship-provisions-in-latest-setback-to-trumps-wide-ranging-election-executive-order).

But the administration has indicated it will continue. In late February 2026, Trump [posted on Truth Social](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/trump-there-will-be-voter-i-d-for-the-midterm-elections-whether-approved-by-congress-or-not/): "There will be Voter I.D. for the Midterm Elections, whether approved by Congress or not!" The DOJ's Civil Rights Division has been [redirected from its original mission to focus on "election integrity"](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/17/politics/midterm-elections-trump-2026-analysis) — using the Civil Rights Act to pressure states to "clean" voter rolls, a move a judge recently ruled was a misapplication of the law.

Election experts are [unanimous that the president cannot legally cancel elections](https://www.votebeat.org/2026/01/26/why-trump-cant-cancel-2026-midterm-elections/). The system is administered by more than 9,000 local jurisdictions and 90,000 polling locations. State and local officials are committed to carrying out the elections they are legally required to run. Courts have been clear that certification is not discretionary.

But the hedge does not require canceling the election. It requires degrading the conditions under which the election produces accountability. If enough procedural chaos is generated — voter re-registration requirements, voting machine changes, legal challenges to ballot counting, ICE presence at polling places — the election can occur and still fail to deliver the oversight that is the one force that unwinds the settlements. The hedge does not need to prevent the election. It needs to prevent the election from functioning as a check.

## V. The distraction as byproduct

A note on framing. This piece deliberately avoids the claim that the administration is "creating chaos on purpose to distract the public." That claim is unnecessary.

When you fire 317,000 federal employees, gut agencies, issue executive orders at unprecedented pace, remove inspectors general in the middle of the night, centralize the Department of Justice under political control, float canceling elections, and restructure the civil service — the chaos generates its own cover. The press covers each event as a discrete story. The public processes each one as a separate outrage. The pattern is too large and moves too fast to be seen by anyone processing the events one at a time. Every move that serves the hedge also serves some other stated purpose — efficiency, accountability, election integrity, government reform — providing cover that is not manufactured but organic. The structural analysis does not require that anyone be aware of the pattern for the pattern to operate. The federal employee who processes a reclassification, the career official who executes a redaction under guidance from the political layer above, the local election administrator who implements a new voter-verification requirement — none of them need to understand the pattern. They need only to follow the procedure.

*"It's only because of their stupidity that they're able to be so sure of themselves."*

Kafka wrote that in *The Trial* — a book about a man arrested, prosecuted, and destroyed by a system whose charges are never disclosed, whose rules are never explained, and whose logic is impenetrable to the person it consumes. The horror is not cruelty. It is procedure — the machinery operating exactly as designed, indifferent to the individual caught inside it. The stupidity Kafka describes is not ignorance. It is the certainty that comes from never having to see the whole. Each person in the system sees only their piece — the form they process, the directive they follow, the guidance they implement. Their certainty that they are doing their jobs correctly is what makes the system work. The system does not require awareness. It requires compliance. And compliance, in a system redesigned from the top, produces the outcome the designers intended — whether or not anyone below them can see what they are building.
