# The Handshake

**The deal that was never written down.**

---

There is no document.

There is no signed agreement between a president and his creditors. No contract filed in a vault. No memo initialed in a back channel. No recording of the moment when the terms were set and the parties agreed. If such a document existed, someone would have found it. If such a recording existed, someone would have leaked it. The absence is real. And it is the point.

A handshake agreement requires no paper. It requires only that both parties understand the terms — and that the behavior that follows is inexplicable without them.

This series has spent six installments documenting the behavior. The policy concessions. The sanctions relief. The non-congressionally-authorized war that served two foreign creditors simultaneously, launched during the only window that guaranteed the payout. The suppression of FBI interviews naming the president in a child sexual abuse investigation. The institutional dismantlement calibrated to a midterm deadline. The cost — in blood, in economic stability, in democratic function, in the lives of intelligence assets whose names were stored in a dressing room at a resort club.

This final installment does not produce the document. It cannot. What it does is ask the reader to hold the full weight of what has been documented — and to answer, honestly, whether any explanation other than the structural one accounts for all of it simultaneously.

## I. What the classified record holds

The public record is extensive. It fills six installments, hundreds of sources, and thousands of pages of government documents released under an act of Congress that passed 427 to 1. But the public record is, by definition, the portion that has been allowed to become public. Behind it sits a classified record that the series has referenced but cannot access — and that may explain why the behavior documented in these pages is as desperate as it appears.

### The damage assessment

When the FBI [searched Mar-a-Lago in August 2022](https://www.npr.org/2022/08/29/1119889338/after-mar-a-lago-officials-are-analyzing-the-potential-damage-to-national-securi) and recovered 184 classified documents — including [HCS material](https://www.npr.org/2022/08/29/1119889338/after-mar-a-lago-officials-are-analyzing-the-potential-damage-to-national-securi) identifying human intelligence sources — a damage assessment was initiated. That assessment would establish the scope of the compromise: which sources were identified in the recovered documents, whether those sources were subsequently captured or killed, and whether the compromise was connected to the [unprecedented CIA cable](https://theweek.com/foreign-affairs/1005694/unusual-cable-specifies-that-dozens-of-cia-informants-have-been-arrested) sent nine months after the documents arrived at the resort.

The assessment has never been made public. The [40-count indictment](https://www.justsecurity.org/86887/national-security-implications-of-trumps-indictment-a-damage-assessment/) was dropped when the subject won the presidency. The classification authority over the assessment now belongs to the man whose residence was searched.

Consider the weight of this. The United States government possesses a document that establishes — to whatever degree the intelligence community has been able to determine — whether a sitting president's handling of classified material contributed to the deaths of people who spied for the United States. That document is classified. The person who controls its classification is the subject of its findings.

If the damage assessment establishes a connection between the Mar-a-Lago documents and the compromised sources, the president is not merely a man with felony convictions and financial entanglements and suppressed FBI interviews. He is a man whose actions may have resulted in the deaths of people who trusted the United States with their lives. The weight of that — the legal, political, and moral weight — is of a different order than anything the public record contains. And it would explain, with a precision that the public record alone does not, why holding power is not a preference but a necessity. Why the hedge is so aggressive. Why the institutional dismantlement is so thorough. Why the urgency is existential rather than political.

### The 37 pages

The [37 missing pages](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/05/nx-s1-5737562/justice-department-missing-epstein-files-trump) of FBI interviews — the ones the DOJ has not produced despite a congressional mandate, a bipartisan investigation, and sustained press attention — are expected to contain [internal communications documenting how the government resolved the accusation](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/doj-releases-epstein-files-containing-sexual-assault-allegations-against-trump) of sexual abuse by a minor against a man who later became president. Not the accuser's account — three summaries of that have already been released. The internal communications. The record of what the government knew, when it knew it, who was told, and what was decided.

If those pages document a decision to suppress a credible accusation — to close the matter without action, for reasons that were political rather than evidentiary — they are not missing pages. They are the most dangerous pages in the federal records system. They would establish that the government of the United States had knowledge of a credible accusation against a future president and chose not to act. That knowledge, in the hands of any foreign intelligence service that also held it, would be leverage of a kind that makes financial entanglement look trivial.

### The surveillance material

The FBI seized [hundreds of gigabytes of data](https://medium.com/@lovefoods_54026/the-jeffrey-epstein-case-surveillance-data-storage-and-network-protocols-0b0c4955f7c0) from Epstein's properties — cameras, recordings, servers, hard drives, CDs. The [Israeli government installed its own surveillance equipment](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/19/israel-installed-security-at-epsteins-manhattan-apartment-for-ex-pm-barak) at the Manhattan apartment, with remote access capabilities. An [FBI memorandum](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/9/what-were-jeffrey-epsteins-links-to-israel) recorded a source's account that the operator was a co-opted intelligence asset. Part II documented all of this.

What Part II also documented — and what matters for this final installment — is that this was one known apparatus. The structural logic of this series suggests it is unlikely to be the only one. And this particular apparatus was connected to a man who [flew on the operator's plane at least eight times](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/latest-epstein-document-release-includes-multiple-trump-mentions). Who [publicly denied ever being on it](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/12/23/newly-released-epstein-files-spotlight-trumps-past-jet-trips-with-maxwell). Whose name appears in [suppressed FBI interviews](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/24/nx-s1-5723968/epstein-files-trump-accusation-maxwell) documenting an accusation of sexual abuse of a minor. And who now holds the presidency of the United States.

The FBI holds whatever was recovered. The foreign government that operated its own equipment holds whatever that equipment collected. The disposition of this material — who has it, who has seen it, and what it shows — has never been publicly established.

This series has been careful to note that the FBI memo is a single source and does not constitute proof. But the question this final installment asks is different from the evidentiary question. It is: does the behavior documented in Parts I through VI make more sense if this material exists, or if it doesn't?

If recordings exist involving the president — and if that material is held by foreign intelligence services documented in this series as having installed and operated the collection equipment — then the behavior is not a mystery. It is a closing. The creditors hold the instruments. The debtor holds the office. The settlements are the policy. And the handshake is the understanding, never spoken aloud, that everyone at the table knows what everyone else has.

## II. The behavior as proof

In criminal law, there is a concept called consciousness of guilt. A defendant who flees, who destroys evidence, who lies about their whereabouts, who intimidates witnesses — their behavior is admissible as evidence of their awareness that they have committed a crime. The jury is instructed that they may, but are not required to, infer guilt from the behavior.

The pattern with this president is older than his political career. In 1973, the Department of Justice [sued Trump and his father](https://www.npr.org/2016/09/29/495955920/donald-trump-plagued-by-decades-old-housing-discrimination-case) for racial discrimination in their apartment buildings — employees were instructed to mark applications from Black tenants with a "C" for "colored" and to tell them no apartments were available. Trump's response was not to address the evidence. It was to [countersue the government for $100 million](https://www.npr.org/2016/09/29/495955920/donald-trump-plagued-by-decades-old-housing-discrimination-case), claim persecution, and ultimately sign a consent decree without admitting wrongdoing. In 1989, five Black and Latino teenagers were wrongfully convicted of assaulting a jogger in Central Park. Trump [took out full-page ads in four New York newspapers](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/06/18/nyregion/central-park-five-trump.html) calling for the death penalty. When DNA evidence exonerated them in 2002 and the city paid a [$41 million settlement](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/06/18/nyregion/central-park-five-trump.html), Trump [called the settlement "a disgrace"](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/06/18/nyregion/central-park-five-trump.html) and continued to insist on their guilt. The pattern — deny the evidence, attack anyone who presents it, never concede, never apologize, treat the admission of wrongdoing as a greater threat than the wrongdoing itself — was visible fifty years before the presidency. It was not instinct. It was instruction. The attorney who represented Trump in the 1973 housing discrimination case was [Roy Cohn](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/06/20/us/politics/roy-cohn-trump-mccarthy.html) — Senator McCarthy's former chief counsel, a man whose operating philosophy Trump later [summarized in three rules](https://www.vanityfair.com/news/2017/06/donald-trump-roy-cohn-relationship): attack, counterattack, and never apologize. Cohn taught Trump that the legal system is not a search for truth. It is a contest of will — and the person who refuses to yield, regardless of the evidence, wins by exhaustion. The behavioral signature documented in this series is not a response to the presidency's pressures. It is the Cohn method, applied at every scale Trump has ever operated at, for half a century.

It scaled with the stakes. When Trump University was sued for fraud, Trump [attacked the presiding judge's Mexican heritage](https://www.cnn.com/2018/04/19/politics/trump-university-settlement/index.html) as evidence of bias, settled for [$25 million](https://www.cnn.com/2018/04/19/politics/trump-university-settlement/index.html) without admitting wrongdoing, and moved on. When the Trump Foundation was investigated by the New York Attorney General, it was [ordered dissolved](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/11/07/nyregion/trump-charities-new-york.html) after the court found a "shocking pattern of illegality" — including using charitable funds to settle personal business debts and purchase a portrait of himself.

It continued into the presidency. When the Mueller investigation examined Russian interference, Trump [attempted to fire the special counsel](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-trump-russia-obstruction/mueller-report-details-multiple-episodes-of-potential-trump-obstruction-idUSKBN1RU24P/), directed White House counsel Don McGahn to create a false record denying it, and [publicly dangled pardons](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/obstruction-justice-mueller-report-702-page-reading-guide) for witnesses who refused to cooperate. The Mueller report documented [ten episodes of potential obstruction](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/obstruction-justice-mueller-report-702-page-reading-guide). When his personal attorney Michael Cohen was investigated, Trump [publicly attacked him](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-us-canada-45246388) until Cohen cooperated with prosecutors, then praised his other attorney for ["refusing to break."](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-us-canada-45246388) When the classified documents were subpoenaed at Mar-a-Lago, his [own attorneys were caught on surveillance camera](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/read-the-federal-indictment-of-donald-trump-in-the-classified-documents-case) moving boxes out of a storage room the day after the subpoena was served.

Deny. Attack the investigator. Reward silence. Punish cooperation. Never admit. When the evidence is subpoenaed, move the boxes. This is not a political strategy. It is the behavioral signature of a person who has operated this way for half a century, across every domain — real estate, charity, education, law, politics, and now the presidency. The behavior documented in this series is not a departure from the pattern. It is the pattern operating at the scale of American foreign policy, with the stakes raised from personal legal exposure to the full architecture described in Parts I through VI.

This series has documented the following behavior:

A president who [fought harder to suppress the Epstein files](https://www.cnn.com/2025/09/03/politics/transparency-epstein-trump-administration) than any foreign policy position — labeling support for disclosure ["a very hostile act to the administration"](https://www.cnn.com/2025/09/03/politics/transparency-epstein-trump-administration) — and who signed the bill only when defeat was inevitable, [without cameras, without ceremony, after clearing the room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epstein_Files_Transparency_Act).

A Justice Department that [withheld FBI interviews](https://www.npr.org/2026/02/24/nx-s1-5723968/epstein-files-trump-accusation-maxwell) naming the president in a sexual abuse accusation, blamed it on a ["coding error"](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/05/nx-s1-5737562/justice-department-missing-epstein-files-trump), and has not produced the remaining 37 pages.

A president who stated publicly ["I was never on Epstein's Plane"](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/12/23/newly-released-epstein-files-spotlight-trumps-past-jet-trips-with-maxwell) when prosecutors' records document [at least eight flights](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/latest-epstein-document-release-includes-multiple-trump-mentions).

An administration that [launched a war without congressional authorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Strait_of_Hormuz_crisis) that simultaneously served the strategic objectives of two foreign governments documented throughout this series as holding proximity to the president's exposure — during the only window that guaranteed both creditors would collect before oversight arrived.

An administration that [rewarded Russia with sanctions relief](https://thehill.com/policy/energy-environment/5782247-trump-lifts-russian-oil-sanctions/) six days after the confirmation that Russia was [providing targeting intelligence to the enemy](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/03/06/russia-iran-intelligence-us-targets/) — intelligence that contributed to the deaths of American service members — and called the intelligence-sharing ["not really a factor."](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/7/us-downplays-reports-russia-gave-iran-intel-to-help-tehran-strike-us-assets)

An institutional dismantlement — [17 inspectors general fired](https://www.npr.org/2025/01/25/g-s1-44771/trump-fires-inspectors-general), [317,000 federal employees removed](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-10/federal-workforce-s-toll-after-a-year-of-doge-and-trump-317-000), the [DOJ's Public Integrity Section gutted](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/department-justices-broken-accountability-system), [50,000 career civil servants reclassified](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/04/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-creates-new-federal-employee-category-to-enhance-accountability/) to at-will status — calibrated to a midterm deadline and targeting, with documented precision, every agency involved in the oversight of the arrangements described in this series.

A co-conspirator — the only person convicted of conspiring with Epstein — [seeking clemency](https://time.com/7375277/ghislaine-maxwell-epstein-trump-clinton-innocent-congress-testimony-clemency-pardon/) from the president and offering to ["clear his name"](https://time.com/7375277/ghislaine-maxwell-epstein-trump-clinton-innocent-congress-testimony-clemency-pardon/) — an offer that reveals, by its existence, that the material she holds is capable of doing the opposite.

And a president who has [floated canceling the midterm elections](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/17/politics/midterm-elections-trump-2026-analysis), whose allies circulated a [draft executive order](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/trump-says-hes-not-mulling-a-draft-executive-order-to-seize-control-over-elections-heres-what-we-know) seizing emergency power over election machinery, and whose administration has indicated it will impose voter identification requirements ["whether approved by Congress or not."](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/trump-there-will-be-voter-i-d-for-the-midterm-elections-whether-approved-by-congress-or-not/)

This is the behavior. All of it is documented. All of it is sourced. None of it requires a leaked memo or a secret recording to verify. The question is what it proves.

## III. The two explanations

This series has, from the beginning, asked the reader to evaluate two explanations for the same set of documented facts.

**The conventional explanation** requires the reader to accept that the behavior described above is the product of coincidence, incompetence, ideology, and ordinary political self-interest — operating independently, across multiple domains, in the same direction, serving the same beneficiaries, within the same finite window, by accident.

That a president with no consistent strategic doctrine happened to launch a war that served two foreign creditors simultaneously. That the timing — during the only window before oversight arrives — is coincidental. That the sanctions relief for a country helping kill American troops reflects a legitimate policy judgment about energy markets. That the suppression of FBI interviews naming the president in a child sexual abuse investigation is a tagging error. That the institutional dismantlement targeting every oversight mechanism relevant to the arrangements described in this series is a governance philosophy. That the co-conspirator's clemency offer reveals nothing about what she holds. That the fight over the Epstein files — harder than any foreign policy fight — reflects nothing about what the files contain. That the damage assessment remains classified for reasons of national security rather than personal survival.

The conventional explanation does not require you to believe that any single one of these is impossible. It requires you to believe that all of them are unrelated.

**The structural explanation** requires only one assumption: that the parties at the table can read the same documents, assess the same exposure, and act in rational self-interest.

The handshake does not require a meeting. It does not require a phone call. It does not require a single moment when the terms were agreed. It requires only that a man with 34 felony convictions, decades of financial entanglement with Russian capital, documented flight records on a sex trafficker and alleged foreign intelligence asset's plane, suppressed FBI interviews naming him in a child sexual abuse accusation, and a classified damage assessment sealed under his own authority — understands what the parties who hold this information want. And that they understand what he needs.

The reader should pause on the phrase "sex trafficker and alleged foreign intelligence asset" — because it forecloses the defense that has been offered for every powerful person named in these files: that Epstein was merely a well-connected wealthy man and that proximity to him was unremarkable. Well-connected wealthy people do not operate multi-property surveillance networks with equipment installed by foreign governments. They are not described by the FBI's own sources as co-opted intelligence agents. The proximity was not social. It was operational. And the man documented in those flight logs now holds the presidency.

The incentives are the terms. The behavior is the handshake. And the policy is the receipt.

## IV. The design flaw

This series opened, in Part I, with an observation about institutional design: the United States vests sole, unreviewable authority over its nuclear arsenal in a single individual. No approval mechanism. No required consultation. No recall.

That system was designed for a president whose personal incentives were, however imperfectly, aligned with the national interest. It was designed on the assumption that the screening mechanisms — elections, background checks, the political process itself — would prevent a person with the kind of exposure documented in this series from reaching the office. It was designed for a world in which the person holding the most consequential authority in human history did not also hold the most documented personal reasons to ensure that the institutional order never holds them accountable.

The assumption failed. The screening mechanisms did not catch a man with 34 felony convictions, because he was convicted after the screening occurred. They did not catch the financial entanglements, because the financial disclosure system is voluntary and incomplete. They did not catch the intelligence exposure, because the damage assessment is classified. They did not catch the leverage, because the leverage operates through incentives, not documents — through handshakes, not contracts.

But the final screening mechanism was never the background check or the disclosure form. It was the voter. The system's last safeguard is the assumption that the electorate will not choose a person whose exposure makes them controllable by foreign creditors — that the public will recognize the risk and reject it. That assumption requires an informed electorate. It requires that the information necessary to make the judgment is available, comprehensible, and not buried under the noise documented in Part V. It requires, in other words, exactly the conditions that every element of the hedge was designed to prevent. The screening mechanism is the voter. The hedge is the mechanism for ensuring the voter cannot screen.

This is not a prediction that the authority will be misused. It is an observation that the system has no mechanism to prevent its misuse by a person with the specific profile documented in these pages. The safeguard is the character of the individual. The series has spent seven installments documenting the character of the individual.

The design flaw is not theoretical. It is operational. It is active. And it is vested in the person this series has described — a man with more documented exposure than any president in modern history, more incentive to maintain power than any predecessor, and fewer institutional constraints on his exercise of it than at any point in his tenure. The question of what happens if the deal falls apart — if the midterms produce oversight, if the files keep coming, if the settlements are not completed in time — is not answered by the institutional safeguards. It is answered by the character of the man who holds the authority. And the character is documented.

## V. The weight of what cannot be said

There is a version of this series that cannot be written. Not because the facts are unavailable, but because the most consequential facts are classified — sealed behind the same authority held by the person they describe.

The damage assessment. The full content of the 37 pages. The surveillance material held by the FBI and by the foreign intelligence services that operated collection equipment inside the properties. The intelligence community's own equities in the broader function. The full scope of what the screening mechanisms missed, and why.

This series has worked with what is public. It has been careful — sometimes painstakingly so — to distinguish between what is documented and what is inferred, between what is sourced and what is structural, between what is known and what the pattern implies. It has declined to assert things it cannot prove. It has noted the limits of single-source evidence. It has attempted to engage the strongest counterarguments — whether it succeeded is for the reader to judge.

But the reader should understand what that discipline costs. The version of this story that includes the classified record — the damage assessment, the full surveillance material, the intelligence equities, the 37 pages, and whatever else exists that this series cannot name because it has never been acknowledged — is not a different story. It is this story with the weight restored. Everything documented in these pages is the portion of the pattern that was allowed to become visible. The portion that was not allowed — the portion sealed under the authority of the man at the center of the closing — is, by the structural logic of this series, the portion that matters most.

The handshake is the deal that was never written down. The classified record is the weight that was never allowed to be felt. And the distance between what the public knows and what the parties at the table know is the space in which the closing operates.

## The shape of the handshake

This series began with a premise: that what the United States is experiencing is a closing — a finite window in which accumulated debts are being settled, by parties acting in rational self-interest, through one of the most valuable commodities on earth: the policy of the United States government.

It documented the debts. The criminal exposure. The financial entanglements. The evidentiary record. The suppressed files. It documented the creditors — the foreign governments whose proximity to the exposure is matched only by the favorability of the policy they receive. It documented the apparatus that produced the leverage. The party that co-signed the debtor's obligations. The counterparties on both sides whose structural constraints prevent them from unwinding the deal. The hedge — the systematic dismantlement of every institutional mechanism that could introduce accountability. And the collateral — you. The person who pays for all of it and was never consulted on any of it.

What it has not produced is the handshake itself. The moment. The agreement. The terms, spoken or unspoken, that explain why every thread documented in this series pulls in the same direction at the same time.

It cannot produce that moment because handshake agreements do not produce moments. They produce behavior. And the behavior is the series you have just read.

The reader who has followed these seven installments must now decide what they believe. Not what they can prove — proof requires the classified record, and the classified record is sealed by the person it describes. Not what they hope — hope is not an analytical category. What they believe, given everything that has been documented, sourced, and laid before them.

The conventional explanation says: coincidence. A corrupt president, a chaotic administration, a series of unrelated policy failures and institutional breakdowns that happen to serve the same beneficiaries in the same window.

The structural explanation says: handshake. Not a meeting. Not a memo. An understanding — arrived at by parties who can read the same documents, assess the same exposure, and do the same math. An understanding that requires no coordination because the incentives *are* the coordination. An understanding that leaves no evidence because the evidence is the policy itself.

This series does not tell the reader which explanation to accept. It has laid out the documented facts. It has mapped the incentives. It has asked the questions that the pattern demands. The answer is yours.

But the answer may not matter — and that is the final observation this series offers. Whether the handshake happened or didn't, the consequences documented in these pages are real. The troops are dead. The oil price has moved. The files are suppressed. The institutions are dismantled. The elections are under assault. The leverage is perpetual. The collateral has been seized. The behavior is the behavior, regardless of what produced it. And the cost is the cost, regardless of whether anyone ever admits what it was for.

You will likely never see the handshake. You are already living in its consequences.

And somewhere — in a resort club in Florida, in a consulate in Manhattan, in a ministry in Moscow, in an office in Jerusalem, in a server room whose location has never been disclosed — the material exists. The recordings. The documents. The assessment. The 37 pages. The names on the flight logs and the images on the hard drives and the cables that were sent and the cables that were not. It exists. The people who hold it know what it contains. And not just one person fears its disclosure.

That is the handshake. Not a meeting. Not a memo. The knowledge itself — shared, unspoken, and enforced by the permanent threat of its disclosure.

This series documented one closing. One president. One set of creditors. One set of settlements. The files that have been released — the 3 million pages, the flight logs, the emails, the FBI memoranda — are the portion that survived suppression, classification, "coding errors," and the resistance of every institution that touched them. They are not the archive. They are what leaked out of it.

The archive is larger. The names are more numerous. The operations are older and wider than one man's presidency. The Epstein apparatus was one known collection platform, operated for two decades, connected to at least one foreign intelligence service and likely others, capturing individuals across both parties, multiple governments, and every level of institutional power documented in these pages. It collapsed because the operator died. The platforms that did not collapse — the ones still running, still collecting, still placing instruments — are, by definition, the ones you cannot see.

This series was the tip.

You were told this was a democracy. You were told the institutions would hold. You were told that the system had safeguards — that no one person could bend the machinery of the most powerful nation on earth to serve their own survival at the expense of everyone else's.

The safeguard was you. And you were never given the information you needed to do the job.
